What Numina is

And how to read this book

Numina is the plural of numen: the sacred presences, the many divine forces. It is the name of this method because that is exactly what you are going to discover in yourself: you are not a single thing. You carry twelve forces within, twelve archetypes, twelve energies, twelve notes, and the capacity to connect with seven planes that you will learn to invoke. Numina names what already dwells in you.

The term "numen" comes from Carl Jung, who took the numinous from Rudolf Otto to name the direct experience of the sacred: that awe that runs through you and fascinates you at the same time. To come face to face with an archetype, or with your deepest Self, is a numinous experience. And that encounter has a name in this book: the 13th Chord, your Luminous Self. Numina, moreover, carries within it the echo of luminous. The method names its own destiny.

The thesis, in one sentence

Numina takes your discontent "your shadows" as a compass, carries you through the four levels of perception integrating your twelve archetypes, from shadow to light, and, generating Love, awakens the 13th note: your Luminous Self, outside of time.

In three steps easy to remember: pain disturbs → Love activates → the 13th Chord emerges. Everything that follows, every concept, every animal, every archetype, every breath and every mantra, serves that sentence. It is not a loose toolbox: it is a single path.

Foundation 01

Pain as a compass

It did not come to punish you. It came to point out where coherence is missing: a particular lack of meaning, in something specific or in several things.

There is a feeling that all of us, as human beings, come to feel sooner or later: knowing, beneath the words, that your life is made to be greater than it is. Not out of ambition, but for the very same reason a seed knows it is made to become a tree. I have felt it, and if you are reading this, surely you have too. And it almost always arrives dressed as restlessness, emptiness, frustration, or pain; not even knowing how to explain something, not finding words for what you feel, yet feeling it.

I want to tell you something from the very first page, because it is the key to everything: your pain is not a defect or a punishment. It is information. It is the most honest sign that something in you is out of place, asking to be seen and reordered. The existential psychologist Rollo May said it decades ago: beneath the anxiety of our time there beats, in truth, an emptiness of meaning. Pain is that emptiness speaking to you.

That is why, in Numina, pain is not the enemy: it is the ear, it lets you identify the dissonance, it tells you which note of your being is out of tune or silenced. And it tells you, with precision, at which level to look:

If it hurts to let go, of the past, of a person, of a version of you, the Serpent (the body) is calling you.

If fear hurts, disguised as prudence, the Jaguar (the mind and the emotions) is calling you.

If the lack of love or meaning hurts, the Hummingbird (the soul) is calling you.

If it hurts not to see yourself, not to know who you are, the Eagle-Condor (the spirit) is calling you.

Pain is the compass: where it hurts, coherence is missing. Not covering it up is the first act of wisdom. Listening to it is the second.

The crisis of meaning, Rollo May

Rollo May was one of the fathers of humanistic and existential psychology, that "third force" which, in contrast to psychoanalysis and behaviorism, placed at the center the freedom, the choice, and the search for meaning of the human being. But May added something that others softened: that there is no growth without passing through anguish, the shadow, and the awareness of death. He did not come to console us: he came to give us courage.

His diagnosis of our era is hauntingly current. In Man's Search for Himself he described the central ill of modern human beings: emptiness, feeling hollow, not quite knowing what one feels or what one wants, and loneliness. It is not that we have too many problems; it is that we have lost the meaning that made them bearable. That is the crisis of meaning: an era with everything within reach and, at the same time, disconnected, empty at times. It is the same emptiness that the statistics of anxiety and disconnection measure today, and the same one the Q'ero elders came down to heal.

And here is his great contribution to Numina. For May, anguish is not merely a symptom to be eliminated. He distinguished neurotic anguish from normal or existential anguish, which appears precisely when a value we feel to be essential to our existence is threatened. Put another way: anguish points to what matters most to us. It is the price of freedom and the doorway to growth. Your pain, read this way, is not a breakdown, it is your being warning you that a meaning of yours is at stake.

In one of his last books, The Cry for Myth, May gave the key that holds up all of this work: a society without shared myths, without stories that order existence, leaves people without a compass, and from there springs much of their anguish. Numina seeks, in part, to show you what for us truly is a possible and universal path: a living myth, which so many cultures have tried to explain in their own way. Exactly what the Q'ero came to remind us of. The twelve archetypes, the five planes, and our 13th Chord are ways of explaining that universal myth from our personal vision, grounded in personal practice.

The universal myth, Campbell and Jung

Rollo May pointed to the wound, we live without myth; Joseph Campbell found the medicine. After comparing the sacred stories of half the world, Campbell discovered that beneath them all beats one and the same story: the hero's journey. Someone receives a call, crosses the threshold into the unknown, passes through trials and a dark night, and returns transformed, bringing a gift for their people. It is the path of Buddha Shakyamuni when he decides to leave his palace, after confronting realities he had never seen, in order to learn on his own two feet. He called it the monomyth: a single myth with a thousand faces. Each culture dresses it differently; the pattern is the same. So is your life.

Campbell drew from Carl Jung, who had already named the wellspring: the collective unconscious. Beneath your personal biography there is a ground common to all of humanity, peopled with archetypes, figures and patterns you did not learn, that you inherited by the sole fact of being human. That is why the same myth appears, almost identical, among peoples who never knew one another. They did not copy it: they remember it. It springs from the same depths.

Here the circle of this book closes. The prophecy of the Eagle and the Condor that the Q'ero brought down from the mountain is not an exotic belief I am asking you to adopt: it is one more face of that universal myth, told by the people who preserved it best. Numina invites you to make it conscious, to embrace that myth, hand in hand with the Inca, and to recognize in your own pain the call of the hero. Campbell summed it up in three words that could be the motto of the whole path: follow your bliss.

I lived it in my own flesh. Each of my losses, and there were many, pushed me, without my knowing it, toward a new level of myself. I am not asking you to love your pain. I am asking you to stop fleeing from it long enough to ask it: what did you come to show me?

That question is the first doorway of the path.

A note of care: working with pain does not replace professional support. If you are passing through a very deep wound, also seek out a therapist or someone you trust. This path is walked better when held.

Foundation 02

Purpose is woven

It is not found. And it is built 80% on the inside, 20% on the outside.

Your purpose is not a hidden thing that one day you will find if you search well. It is not buried, waiting for the moment to reveal itself. It is something that is woven: with genuine steps, sustained over time, until the direction stops being an aspiration and becomes identity. Purpose is not found by sitting still: it finds you while you move.

And here is the most liberating, and most uncomfortable, truth of personal growth: any meaningful goal is 80% psychology and 20% mechanics. The mechanics are the plan, the technique, the steps: they matter, they are the 20%. But most people do not fail for lack of strategy, but because of the other 80%: their beliefs, their inner state, their identity, the story they tell themselves about what is possible for them.

The 20%, Mechanics

Plan, technique, steps, resources. Necessary, but insufficient on its own. It is what you do.

The 80%, Psychology

Beliefs, state, emotion, coherence, identity. It decides whether the mechanics get executed or abandoned. It is who you are while doing it.

That is why Numina works in that order. First the 80%, your state, your coherence, your integrated shadow; only then the action. Not because action does not matter (it matters enormously), but because the action that is born of coherence is of another nature: sustainable, fluid, aligned. The one born of fear burns out.

You do not have a strategy problem. You have a state problem. Fix the state, and the strategy you already know begins to work.

And a myth worth letting go of once and for all: that working harder equals having more. Effort is not the currency. There are those who row their whole lives against their own current. When you weave from your truth, abundance arrives as a consequence, not as a prize for suffering. Purpose and prosperity are not bought with exhaustion: they are cultivated with coherence.

The 80/20 is not a laboratory figure: it is a compass for where to put your energy. Most of your leverage lies in the place where you try the least.

Foundation 03

Your state creates your chemistry

Your beliefs beget your biochemistry, and your biochemistry runs your life

If psychology is 80%, it pays to know how it works on the inside. It works like this: your energy, the sum of your beliefs, creates your chemistry. Your chemistry creates your state. And your state runs your behavior. Changing your life from behavior is rowing against the current. Changing it from belief is changing the current itself.

Belief (energy). What you hold to be true about yourself and the world. Not your surface phrases: your deep convictions, many of them recorded before the age of seven.

Emotion and chemistry. Every belief lights up an emotion, and every emotion is a real chemical recipe. Your body bathes in the chemistry of what you believe.

State. That sustained chemistry becomes your default state: contraction or expansion, protection or growth.

Behavior. From that state you act, or you don't. And action produces your result.

Science backs it up. Bruce Lipton's epigenetics showed that genes do not determine your destiny: the environment, including your inner environment of thoughts and emotions, decides which genes are expressed. And your brain, moreover, is plastic: as early as the nineteenth century, Santiago Ramón y Cajal, the father of neuroscience and a Nobel laureate, intuited it with a phrase that today is science, «every man can be, if he so chooses, the sculptor of his own brain». Every thought you repeat with emotion is a chisel stroke: neurons that fire together wire together, and so your mental habits rewrite, little by little, your very own wiring. And Joe Dispenza turned it into a practical equation: clear thought + elevated emotion + coherent state = biological change. The brain does not clearly distinguish between a vividly imagined experience and a real one; when you add emotion, it marks it as real. A belief repeated with emotion is not a pretty phrase: it is a biological instruction.

And there are those who take this idea down to its root. The physicist Thomas Campbell, in My Big TOE, his «big theory of everything», proposes that consciousness comes first and that reality works like an information system, something like a simulation, in which the future is not fixed but exists in a state of probabilities. And what tips the scales of the possible? Your intention and your inner state. For Campbell, to grow means to lower your entropy (in physics, entropy is the measure of a system's disorder or chaos), that is, to order your inner world, release fear and move your consciousness toward love; and in doing so the probabilities that materialize in your life literally change. It is the same truth of the Q'ero spoken in the language of a physicist: tune your inner energy and you rewrite what the world gives back to you. It is the same one I have lived in my own skin, and that is why I share moments of my life with you: so that you see it is not myth, it is real.

You do not act according to what you want. You act according to what you believe possible for yourself. Change the belief, and you change, literally, the chemistry from which you live.

The coherent heart, HeartMath

Here enters the most underrated organ of your transformation: the heart. It is not just a pump. It has its own nervous system, some forty thousand neurons, a small «brain» of its own, and it sends the brain more signals than it receives from it. That is to say: to a large extent, the heart tells the brain what state to be in, and not the other way around. That is why, when you want to change your chemistry, the fastest path does not go through the head: it goes through the heart.

HeartMath research measured it in the heart rhythm. When you live in stress, fear or frustration, the heartbeats form an irregular and chaotic pattern, incoherent, and that also disorders the brain: you think worse and react more. But when you feel gratitude, appreciation or care, the rhythm of the heart becomes smooth and orderly, like a harmonious wave. That state is called heart coherence: heart, breath and brain beating in sync. It is, in the language of Numina, the Alpha state, the 80%, the energy from which everything else becomes possible.

And the simplest key to entering coherence is gratitude. Not the courteous kind, but the kind truly felt in the chest. Activating genuine appreciation calms the central nervous system, lowers cortisol, the stress hormone, raises DHEA, the vitality one, and returns balance to the body in a matter of minutes. Gratitude is not just a virtue: it is a physiological technology, the kindest way to change your chemistry.

The vagus nerve, the calm button

Behind all of this is the vagus nerve: the great nerve of rest and connection, the «brake» of the nervous system, the very one embodied by the magnetic Condor. The better your vagal tone, the faster you move from fear to calm. Stephen Porges's polyvagal theory describes three states: that of safety and connection (ventral vagal), that of fight or flight (sympathetic) and that of shutdown or freezing (dorsal vagal). Breathing slowly, especially lengthening the exhalation, feeling gratitude and focusing on the heart stimulate the vagus and return you to safety. That is why box breathing and heart coherence are not pretty rituals: they are physiological buttons for coming home. And the most current science backs this up.

The physiotherapist Antonio Valenzuela, with a master's in psychoneuroimmunology, devotes an entire book to the subject, Estimula tu nervio vago (Stimulate Your Vagus Nerve), and his message is clear: good vagal tone regulates stress, improves digestion and lowers inflammation, the kind that pulses beneath so many chronic illnesses. And the most beautiful thing for us is that the keys he proposes are slow breathing with a good long exhalation, singing, gratitude, laughter, and even caring for the microbiota of that gut which is our «second brain». Remember: the vagus nerve is precisely what unites the three centers, belly, heart and mind, into a single conversation. Returning to it is not fleeing the world: it is giving the body the safety from which, only then, it can heal. Cutting-edge science and the grandparents point, once again, to the very same door.

How I came to HeartMath

In fact, the first time I heard of HeartMath was in 2014, years before training at this wonderful center, in El maestro del corazón (The Master of the Heart), a beautiful book by Annie Marquier. Those pages planted the seed in me: the idea that the heart has an intelligence of its own and that returning to it was the path. This is not borrowed theory: I am a HeartMath® Certified Mentor in Building Personal Resilience, and I have accompanied many people, and myself, to find in the heart the switch that the mind alone could not locate.

The magic of believing, a word for women

There is a small, old book, The Magic of Believing, by Claude M. Bristol, written in 1948, which says in plain words what science would confirm decades later: that belief sustained with feeling is a force that organizes your life. Bristol called it the science of belief, and he devoted an entire chapter, nearly a century before it was fashionable, to women. His thesis strikes me as poetic justice: that precisely because of her intuition, her constancy and her deep capacity to feel, woman has a natural gift for this force, when she stops using it against herself.

Because that is where the trap lies. With the same intensity with which a woman can believe in her dream and give life to a new human being, with that same intensity she often pours it into believing in her own unworthiness, in the «I can't», in the «it's not for me». The energy is the same; only what you believe in changes. Bristol did not ask for forced positive thinking: he asked you to engrave on the mind, with emotion and repetition, the image of what you want to be, until the subconscious takes it as truth and begins to weave it into the concrete.

The mirror technique, Bristol

One of his most powerful practices, and one of the oldest known:

1. In front of the mirror. Look yourself in the eyes, standing, like someone who looks at a person they truly respect.

2. Speak in the present and with feeling. Tell yourself, out loud and as if it were already true, what you need to believe: «I am capable», «I deserve this», «I am achieving it».

3. Repeat until you feel it. Don't count the times: repeat until the chest believes it, not just the mouth. It is emotion that engraves.

Do it every morning. It is not vanity or self-deception: it is training your subconscious to sustain the state from which your life reorganizes itself.

You already tried it on the first page; here you understand why it works. It is the master key of the method, the one that lights up the heart center within minutes.

Heart coherence, in 3 steps

1. Attention to the heart. Bring your attention to the center of the chest, to the area of the heart.

2. Breathe through the heart. Breathe a little slower and deeper, as if the air entered and left through there, about 5 seconds on the inhale and 5 on the exhale.

3. Activate appreciation. Bring to the chest a genuine feeling of gratitude or affection, a person, a place, a memory, and hold it for a few minutes.

Three minutes are enough to begin changing your chemistry. It is the perfect prelude to any Numina practice, and the direct bridge to Munay.

From here come three of your laws of life. What you focus on expands (your attention is the watering can: what you water, grows, so look at what is here, not what is missing). You deserve to have it all, not for what you do, but for what you are; worthiness is a state, and it can be healed. And be a great believer and optimist of your dream: faith does not change the laws of physics, but it changes what you dare to begin.

Foundation 04

The Eagle and the Condor

Your two wings: neither flies alone. And where this prophecy descends from.

It is the prophecy the Q'ero kept, and it is the spine of this entire book. The Eagle, the north, the mind, science, doing; and the Condor, the south, the heart, intuition, being. For five hundred years the Eagle dominated and the Condor almost disappeared. But the time is coming when both fly together again. This is not poetry: it is a precise diagnosis in symbolic language.

Where this descends from, the Q'ero

High in the Peruvian Andes, near 4,500 meters, lives a Quechua people considered among the last direct keepers of Inca wisdom: the Q'ero. When the conquistadors came, they did not fight: they climbed higher. And there, in silence, for five hundred years, they preserved a prophecy, that of a pachakuti, an overturning of the world in which harmony would be restored and chaos and disconnection would end.

In the mid-twentieth century they came down from the mountain; «we have been waiting for you for five hundred years», the crowd told them. Their wisdom began to open to the world through teachers like Alberto Villoldo, who trained among them (and with whom I have had the wonderful fortune to train in his school; I recommend him to every seeker of truth).

And from the Quechua word ayllu, community, the name of this system is born: DAyllu, the digital ayllu we weave with you today.

The heart of their prophecy is this image of the Eagle and the Condor. Look at the data of our era (the anxiety, the loneliness, the lack of meaning) and you will see that the grandfathers were right: we have lived the flight of a single wing. Numina is, at its core, a way of learning to fly with both. I take this wisdom with reverence and weave it together with the psychology of the archetypes and the neurosciences available today, to place it in your hands, here, now.

The Eagle, electric

Sympathetic nervous system (action). Analytical hemisphere. Doing, producing, projecting. Logic and strategy: the mechanics.

The Condor, magnetic

Parasympathetic nervous system (restoration). Intuitive hemisphere. Being, feeling, receiving. Heart coherence: the state.

The «Eagle without Condor» is the air we breathe: efficiency without compassion (the epidemic of exhaustion), action without rest (the anxiety), thinking without feeling (the disconnection). It is the mechanics running without the state that sustains it, exactly the stagnation we were talking about. But the opposite trap exists too: the «Condor without Eagle» is intuition without form, empathy without limits, flow without manifesting. Much well-meaning spirituality stays there: plenty of state, no work.

With a single wing you fly in circles until you fall. Mastery is not choosing one wing: it is contextual flexibility, knowing how much Eagle and how much Condor each moment asks for.

This resolves the false fight between «working on your inner self» and «taking action». It is not one or the other. A negotiation perhaps asks for more Eagle than Condor; a friend in crisis, far more Condor than Eagle; creation, above all Condor with a touch of Eagle. There is no fixed proportion. Wisdom is flexibility. Keep this question for the whole of your life: does this ask today for more Eagle or more Condor?

And notice the elegance: the Eagle-Condor appears at two scales within Numina. As a level of perception (the spirit/higher self, creator, universe… «whatever you call it», we will see it in Part II) and as a polarity that organizes your twelve archetypes into six electric radiators and six magnetic balancers (Part III). The reunion of the two wings is the principle that repeats at every scale of your being.

And it is worth saying from the start: this prophecy is not only of the Andes. It is one of the many faces of a single universal myth (that of the self who falls, fragments, and returns home transformed) that Joseph Campbell found beneath every story and Jung explained as the common inheritance of the soul. I take the Andean voice, the most alive and the closest to the earth and to me as a South American woman, as a doorway to a myth that belongs to us all.

Foundation 05

You were born tuned to a single note

But the twelve live within you. That is the great realization.

You are born tuned to one note: your imprint, the archetype that sounds in you effortlessly, according to your year of birth. It is your starting note. But, and here is the teaching that changes everything, the twelve notes already live within you. They are not exclusively yours: they are human. Jung called it the collective unconscious: the archetypes are patterns that all of humanity shares.

To believe «I am of such-and-such archetype» is like a piano that insists on playing a single key. Your imprint is the door, not the cage. The work of your life is to learn to play the twelve, and to integrate them. That is the great realization of Numina: we come with an imprint, but we all share the twelve, and the goal is to integrate them all.

And when they sound together, not one after another but all at once, something extraordinary happens: you step out of time. That is the 13th Chord: the moment when the twelve become one. The 13 is not a new note nor one more archetype added to the list: it is what appears when the twelve gather and integrate into a single sound. Your unity, your wholeness.

A melody is notes in sequence: that is time. A chord is the same notes at once: that is eternity. Your Luminous Self is the instant when all your music sounds at once, as a single sound.

And there is a second language, that of color, which says the same thing. Your twelve archetypes are also your emotional rainbow: twelve shades, each with its hue of light and of shadow. Just as the twelve notes together form the chord, the twelve colors together form white light. The 13th Chord is white light: all the colors at once. And the rainbow is already sacred in the Andes, the wiphala, the k'uychi: your emotional rainbow is not a borrowed metaphor, it lives in the lineage.

That central point, where the twelve become one, has five names in five lineages, and they all point to the same thing: Jung's Self (the center of the mandala), the complete Andean chakana, the chord in which all the notes sound as one, the white light of the rainbow, and Wiracocha, the luminous being. Jung's mandala and the Andean chakana are the same figure: four directions and a center. It is no coincidence. It is the archetype of the Self appearing identical in cultures without contact, exactly what Jung predicted.

The voice behind the map

Who I am

I'll tell it to you briefly. Not to speak about myself, but so that you may recognize your own story in mine.

I am Natalia. I grew up amid losses and farewells, and even so I never stopped believing that life held something greater for me. I learned early to let go; it would take me years to learn to hold myself. And I understood, in time, that pain never came to break me: it came to show me the way. If something here has resonated with you, it is because what I explain is not theory: I lived it. I'll tell it to you in a single page, and then you become the protagonist again.

My story began with absences. At three years old, my mother had to leave me in my grandmother's care; until I was seven I grew up with neither mother nor father near. The roles got shuffled early: my uncle stood in as father, my grandmother as my anchor. And I lost almost all of them too soon: first my uncle, then my grandmother, later my mother. So many farewells, so soon, forged in me, without my knowing it, my first strength: flexibility. To bend without breaking, to rebuild the ground beneath my feet again and again.

The question that would not let go of me

Before anything else I was the one who asks: I have been asking questions since I was three. That curious girl became, over the years, a very concrete obsession. When Ana Laura, the aunt with whom I grew up as a sister, suffered at 23 a brain abscess that left her without present memory, a question lodged itself in me and never let go: what can be reprogrammed in the human mind, and what cannot?

That question led me to specialize in neurolinguistic programming and to live with a sacred urgency: to make the most of life, now. You too have a question that will not let go of you. It is worth listening to.

The «I don't deserve it» that is inherited

My mother was of a disarming sweetness and a tireless fighter; alone and divorced, she raised me almost without help and went on to become a businesswoman. She had the talent and the dedication. What she lacked was not in her hands, but in a programming inherited from scarcity: that «I don't deserve it» that would not let her hold on to what she built with so much love. She was abused as a child; my grandmother, orphaned at birth, faced a poverty so deep that she had no shoes until she was seven. Wounds and strength travel in silence from one generation to the next. And almost always, behind the «I can't», there is a wound, not a truth. What «I don't deserve it» did you inherit, without ever choosing it?

Hitting bottom to choose again

I too survived abuse, and twice death brushed close by me, at thirteen and at twenty. Each brush returned me with my eyes more open. That near-death experience at twenty changed everything: it returned me to life determined to live it on my own terms. There I understood, for the first time, what is today the heart of my system.

Sometimes you have to touch the edge to remember that you came here to live.

If you don't like your reality, look straight at your own pattern of beliefs: the reality you live is coherent with the pattern you hold today. Observing it, tearing down what limits you, and restructuring it is, believe me, the most important thing you can invest your time in.

Why I devote my life to the heart

Then came losses that split you in two: in 2014 I said goodbye to a son who could not stay; and in 2017, already an emigrant in the United Kingdom after building from scratch for the second time, my mother departed suddenly. PART II

The 4 Levels

Four power animals, four worlds you cross: from body to spirit

The vertical axis

The four worlds

They do not contain your archetypes: they are the perception from which you live them

The four power animals of the Andean tradition are not ornaments: they are four levels of perception, four worlds you cross from the dense to the subtle. They are the way in which you perceive your essence: those twelve potential energies you carry within. And the same twelve are perceived and lived differently depending on the level from which you look, in the body, in the emotion, in the soul that creates or in the spirit that observes.

You climb through them as up a stairway of light: you release in the body (Serpent), you cross mind and emotions (Jaguar), you open the soul (Hummingbird) and you touch the spirit (Eagle-Condor). And each level has its sound, a mantra, and its question. When pain appears, it tells you in which world you are stuck; these four chapters tell you how to cross it.

And there is something that unites ancestral wisdom with neuroscience: each animal corresponds to a brain and to an element. What the elders named in the language of animals, science would describe centuries later in the language of brains:

Serpent, Earth, reptilian brain. Pure survival: fight or flight. Still without emotions, only the body that wants to live.

Jaguar, Water, limbic brain. Emotion and symbolic language. Territorial and defensive; if it feels threatened, it fights.

Hummingbird, Fire, neocortex. It re-creates what it observes: music, art, mathematics, science. The soul that imagines and creates.

Eagle-Condor, Air, prefrontal lobes. Self-observation, transcendence of the self, conscious choice. The witness.

This is not just ancestral poetry: it is, broadly speaking, a map of how your brain was formed, layer upon layer, in the very order in which you travel through these four levels, from the reptilian brain of pure survival to the prefrontal lobes, the newest and most human part, the one that allows you to observe yourself and choose. It is a simplified model, but a revealing one: to climb through the four levels of Numina is to retrace the evolution of your own brain to its summit, that of the witness who chooses.

The medicine wheel and the chakana

To understand all of this as a single body, it helps to look at the map that contains it. Many of the native peoples of America keep one same sacred figure: the medicine wheel, a circle divided into four directions, each with its color, its element, its animal and its dimension of being, the physical, the emotional, the mental and the spiritual. I take it with respect, as inspiration, without pretending to speak for the peoples who keep it.

In the Andes, that same wisdom takes the form of the chakana, the square, stepped cross that the Southern Cross draws in the sky. Its four arms are, in the language of this book, the four animals: Serpent, Jaguar, Hummingbird and Eagle-Condor. And here is the key that unites it all: the wheel and the chakana have a center. The point where the four directions cross is not empty.

That center is the Munay: love, the heart, the force that sustains and reconciles everything else. The four directions are the spokes; the Munay is the still axis from which all depart and to which all return. That is why, in Numina, the coherence of the heart (the one you already tried on the first page) is not one more practice: it is the center of the wheel, the switch of the entire system. If the heart is not in the center, the wheel does not turn. And when the four directions turn around a coherent heart, the whole chakana beats, and the 13th Chord is lit.

The three brains, belly, heart and mind

For the Q'ero people there are three centers of power they consciously honor: the belly, the heart and the mind. And today neuroscience confirms it with astonishment: we do not think only with the head. The gut houses its own network of millions of neurons, the "second brain," seat of instinct. The heart also has its own little brain and sends the head more signals than it receives. And the mind, the brain we all know. Three intelligences, one single person.

And here the two maps embrace. The belly is the Serpent, the body and its instinct; the heart is the Hummingbird and the Munay, the center that sustains all; and the mind ascends from the Jaguar, the emotion that thinks, to the Eagle-Condor, the witness that chooses. To heal, deep down, is to make them sound together: when the belly, the heart and the mind stop contradicting each other and beat in coherence, health appears, and the first spark of the 13th Chord is lit.

A note: sound is the Word set to vibrate. Singing a mantra with elevated emotion trains your state just as the coherence of the heart does. That is why each level brings its own: it is not decoration, it is technology of the soul.

And this is not only Andean poetry: the same ascent appears, under other names, in traditions that never knew one another (the four qualities of Krishnamurti, the four levels of perception of modern consciousness). If you want to see those parallel maps side by side, I gathered them at the end, in Parallel Maps (appendix). Here the essential is enough for us: four animals, four brains, four worlds, one single stairway, and a heart in the center.

Level 1, in depth

The body and addictions

What the body grabs onto when the soul is hungry

Before continuing to climb, let us stay a moment in the Serpent, because here a battle is waged that almost all of us know. The body is the first place where what we do not want to feel lands. And when something hurts and we do not look at it, the body seeks, in its own way, to silence it: with tobacco, with alcohol, with sugar, with work without brakes, with bonds that are not good for us. That is an addiction, not a vice that makes you a bad person, but a strategy of the body to avoid feeling.

We have many, and almost none of them goes by the name of addiction. The cigarette that accompanies anxiety. The drink that loosens the day. The sweet that rewards or consoles. The work that fills the hours so as not to hear the silence. And the most invisible of all: the friendships and bonds that diminish us, those who want us small, those who feed the complaint, those who need us broken to feel in their place. That too is depended upon, and that too is let go of. Every addiction promises the same thing and lies the same way: relief now, in exchange for life later.

There is no addiction to a substance: there is addiction to not feeling. To not wanting to see, to not acknowledging that you do not yet have the tools, and so you choose to scatter yourself, to distract yourself and not to think. Change what you think, and what you feel will change, and the grip loosens on its own.

Here enters a tool that changed my life: The Work of Byron Katie, which she develops in Loving What Is. Her discovery is disarming: we do not suffer because of reality, but because of what we think about it. Behind each craving there is no substance: there is an unquestioned thought, "I need this to calm down," "I cannot handle this without help," "I deserve it." Katie does not ask you to resist the craving with willpower. She asks you something wiser: to question the thought that ignites it.

The Work, applied to the craving

When the impulse arrives, the cigarette, the drink, the sweet, the message to the one who hurts you, do not fight: inquire. Take the thought that sustains it ("I need this") and pass it through four questions:

1. Is it true?

2. Can I know with absolute certainty that it is true?

3. How do I react, what happens in me, when I believe that thought?

4. Who would I be, in this instant, without that thought?

And do the turnaround: "I need this" → "I do not need this," "this thought needs me." See whether the turnaround is as true, or truer, than the original. The craving is neither obeyed nor fought: it is disarmed by looking at it head-on.

The Serpent taught us to shed the old skin. An addiction is an old skin that squeezes and promises warmth. To shed it is not punishment or deprivation: it is to make room. And each time you question the thought instead of obeying the impulse, you give the body back what it was truly asking for, not smoke or sugar, but to be felt, to be inhabited, to be loved.

As in Foundation 01: if the wound is very deep or the dependence very physical, seek professional support as well. This path is best walked supported.

Level 2, West

Jaguar, the mind and the emotions

Beyond fear and death. The path of the shadow.

Element, Water, Limbic brain, Emotion, territory,

The jaguar sees in the dark and walks without fear where others do not dare. It is the guardian of the second level: the mind and the emotions, the territory of the shadow. Here it is not about thinking more prettily, but about looking head-on at what you avoid: fear disguised as prudence, the stories that limit you, the parts of you that you denied.

Fear almost never announces itself as fear: it arrives as caution, as "it is not the moment," as "I need to prepare a little more." It sounds reasonable because it uses your voice. The Jaguar teaches you to distinguish genuine prudence (which produces concrete information) from disguised fear (which only produces more delay). What you deny governs you; what you integrate gives you back its power.

Its element is water: the emotions that drag away or that cleanse. To work this level is to learn to feel without the emotion governing you, and to look head-on at the shadow that fear hides.

Fear is not a stop sign. It is the sign that you have reached the edge of your territory. And the edge is exactly where growth lives.

Level 2, in depth

The mind that heals the body

The placebo, the emotion and that which science still cannot fully explain

If the Jaguar governs the mind and the emotions, here it is time to look head-on at something that changed my life: what we feel and believe does not stay in the head, it descends to the body and, at times, transforms it. It is not magic nor the typical "think positive." It is the constant, real and measurable conversation between the mind, the emotion and biology.

My mother's case

My mother went through something that still leaves me without words. She was diagnosed with a non-Hodgkin lymphoma, a cancer of the lymphatic system, very advanced, at stage IV, with involvement of the cerebrospinal fluid, among other complications that, according to the doctors, gave her about three months to live. She kept on living like someone who carries no sentence. And, against all prognosis, her body achieved a spontaneous remission: the cancer, simply, went away. Years later she would pass, but from a very different illness. Medicine calls it that, "spontaneous remission," which is the honest name science gives to something it still cannot fully explain. I do not claim to know what happened. I only know what I saw: that not carrying the weight of the sentence let her keep on living, and that in that life something dared to look and, somehow, healed.

What science does know: the placebo

And here we are not speaking of faith, but of studies. The placebo effect is one of the most solid proofs that the mind moves the body: when someone truly believes they are receiving a treatment, their body manufactures, all on its own, part of the effects of that treatment. Simulated knee surgeries have been documented that relieved arthrosis just as much as the real ones; faked heart operations that improved 83% of the patients (published in the New England Journal of Medicine); placebo injections that restored mobility to people with Parkinson's. It is not imagination: it is real chemistry, triggered by a belief. (And I do not say it from hearsay: I worked for centers of excellence and for the medicine department of the University of Oxford, in the middle of the pandemic, supporting leading laboratories.) And if a belief can do that without any substance at all, do you realize the power your beliefs hold, and just how far you can work them in your favor?

Those who dared to believe

Joe Dispenza lived it in his own flesh. In 1986, at 23 years old, an off-road vehicle ran him over during a triathlon and broke six vertebrae; four surgeons agreed: an operation with steel rods in the spine or paralysis. He refused the surgery and left the hospital with a single idea: "the power that made the body heals the body." For weeks, two hours in the morning and two at night, he reconstructed his spine in his imagination, vertebra by vertebra. At nine and a half weeks he walked again. Today he researches and teaches that mind-body connection, and he titled one of his books, bluntly, You Are the Placebo.

The scientist and author Gregg Braden adds a piece that touches the Jaguar squarely: the emotion. For him, the language that truly speaks to the body is not thought, but feeling: feeling as if what you long for were already real. Braden often shows a video, recorded in a "medicine-free" hospital in Beijing, where three practitioners hold the sensation that a woman is already healed while an ultrasound records, in those minutes, how her tumor dissolves. It is a unique case, without rigorous scientific verification, and it is best to take it for what it is: a testimony, not a proof. But it points to something that neuroscience does confirm: emotion changes your chemistry, and your chemistry, sustained over time, changes you.

Do not ask the body to heal while your mind repeats the story that makes it sick. Integrate the emotion first, release the sentence, and let the body hear something different.

That is why this is the level of the Jaguar: because almost everything begins in the unlooked-at emotion. The guilt, the "I do not deserve," fear turned into habit, all of that is stagnant water that the body ends up carrying. To integrate the shadow, to feel what we silence and let it flow, is not only a work of the soul: it is also, very often, the beginning of health.

Important, and I say it with my hand on my heart: none of this replaces medicine. If you or someone you love is going through an illness, seek and follow the best medical treatment available; what I share here accompanies it, it does not replace it. The mind is a powerful ally of healing, never an excuse to abandon the care you need.

Level 3, in depth

Listening to your own soul

When the destiny others dream for you is not your own

The Hummingbird is the guardian of the soul, and the soul has a voice of its own, distinct from that of fear and distinct from that of others. Sometimes we confuse it with what is expected of us, with the path that others, even from the most sincere love, traced for our life. But the soul knows its flight. And there is a story, born on a mountain in Granada, that tells it like few others.

The boy who had it all written out

High in the Alpujarras, near Mulhacén, there is a Buddhist retreat center called O Sel Ling, "place of the clear light," which the Dalai Lama himself named. Near there, in the village of Bubión, Osel Hita Torres was born in 1985, son of two disciples of the great Tibetan master Lama Yeshe, founder of one of the most widespread Buddhist traditions in the West. Lama Yeshe had died a year earlier. And when Osel was barely fourteen months old, the Dalai Lama recognized him as his reincarnation. He was enthroned while still almost a baby. At the age of six he was taken to a monastery in India, where he grew up amid prayers and discipline, far from his family, prepared to inherit his master's lineage. He had, quite literally, the highest destiny a tradition can offer, written from the cradle.

The day he chose his own flight

And yet, upon turning eighteen, Osel did something almost no one expected: he set aside the robes and returned to the West to live his own life. He did not renounce what he had learned, on the contrary, he is deeply grateful for it, but he said, with disarming honesty, that he no longer fit into that life, that he felt he was living "something imposed from outside." He studied film, traveled, became a father, and today he devotes much of his energy to planting trees and caring for the earth. He remained spiritual, in his own way, with his own practice. He chose, in the end, to listen to his soul above the role the whole world had assigned to him.

Your soul also knows how to fly

His story moves me because it is the Hummingbird in its purest state. It is not about rejecting those who love us nor the wisdom they gave us; Osel honors his teachers. It is about something more subtle and more courageous: distinguishing between the path others dream for you, even though they love you, and the one your soul recognizes as its own. Almost no one will carry a crown like his, but we all bear others' expectations on our shoulders: those of our parents, our culture, what we were "supposed" to be. The Hummingbird asks the only thing that matters: is this flight mine, or one that was lent to me?

You can be grateful for the nest you were given and, even so, take flight. To honor those who loved you is not to live their dream: it is to dare to live your own, which deep down is what they truly wanted for you.

That is why this is the level of the soul. Here it is not about fleeing nor breaking for the sake of breaking, but about tuning the ear inward until you recognize your own note, the one only you can play, and having the courage to follow it. My mother told me in her own way as she let me go: "follow your intuition, and you will never go wrong." That is the Hummingbird: the bravery to follow your soul, even when the path does not yet have a name.

Level 4, in depth

The force that creates everything

Unconditional love, the language of the spirit

At the very top, where the Eagle and the Condor fly, lives the force that makes life exist and that turns coincidences into something no longer coincidental. It is not proven with arguments: it is recognized when, suddenly, you feel that something greater, intelligent, and loving holds you. At this level it is no longer about believing in that force, but about learning to speak its language. And its language is one alone: love.

One single force, a thousand names

Each culture has named it in its own way. The Andes call it Wiracocha; other traditions say God, the Source, the Great Spirit, the Tao, the Field. The names change; the experience does not. It is the same presence that beats beneath all the stories: the certainty that you are not alone, that you belong to something vast and alive. To recognize it does not make you of any particular religion: it returns you to the common root of them all.

Ho'oponopono, healing the world by healing yourself

There is a Hawaiian tradition, part of the wisdom some call Huna, that takes this idea to its most radical and most beautiful form: Ho'oponopono, "to put things in their place." Its best-known practice fits into four phrases, "I'm sorry, forgive me, thank you, I love you," but its heart is an idea that disarms: to take one hundred percent of the responsibility for what appears in your life and to heal it within you, not outside.

The case is told of Dr. Ihaleakala Hew Len, a psychologist who in the eighties worked for four years in the ward for patients with mental illness and criminal records at the Hawaii State Hospital. He did not practice therapy in the usual way: he reviewed each patient's file and, instead of working on them, he worked on himself, repeating those four phrases, cleaning within himself what he saw outside. Over the years, they say, the ward was transformed: patients who improved, staff who stopped leaving, until the ward closed. It is worth saying honestly: it is a celebrated account, recorded in the book Zero Limits, not a verified clinical study, so we take it as an inspiring testimony, not as proof. But its teaching is profoundly that of the Eagle-Condor: there is no "out there" entirely separate from you; when you heal your gaze with love, what you see changes.

Theta, the frequency of the still soul

And this level leaves its mark on the brain as well. When the mind truly stills, its waves descend to the theta rhythm, that threshold between waking and sleep where the boundaries of the self soften, intuition opens, and the body enters its deepest mode of repair. It is no coincidence that deep prayer, meditation, and healing occur there. Theta is, in the language of neuroscience, the antechamber of unity: the stillness from which unconditional love ceases to be an idea and becomes experience.

This level is not thought: it is loved. The spirit does not understand arguments, it understands love. And when you speak its language, the world, inside and out, begins to reorder itself.

That is why the Eagle and the Condor culminate here, in compassion and unconditional love: not the love that asks, but the love that gives; not the compassion that looks down from above, but the one that recognizes itself in the other. It is the language of the spirit and, not by coincidence, the note that ignites the 13th Chord. To inhabit this level is, at heart, to learn to love without conditions, beginning with yourself.

The practice of Ho'oponopono

Before anything that disturbs you, a person, a piece of news, a memory, instead of fighting with what is outside, turn inward and repeat, truly feeling it: "I'm sorry. Forgive me. Thank you. I love you." You are not speaking to the other: you are speaking to the part of you that still carries that wound. You clean your gaze, and from there, everything begins to change.

Level 4, in depth

The living Earth

Pachamama is not a symbol. She is a being.

Pachamama, Gaia, The Earth's chakras,

From on high, where the Eagle and the Condor fly, you see something that is forgotten below: the Earth is not the stage of our life, it is alive. For the Andes it was never a metaphor: the Pachamama is a mother, a being from whom permission is asked, to whom thanks are given, and to whom something is returned. Science arrived late at the same intuition and called it Gaia: the entire planet behaving like a living organism that self-regulates to sustain life.

Some went further. Robert Coon dedicated his life to mapping the Earth as a living energetic body, with its chakras, centers of energy, distributed across the planet, just as the human body has them. And here something happens that left me shaken: in his map, one of those great centers beats at Lake Titicaca, at the heart of the Andean world. The same land that sheltered the Q'ero is, in this language, one of the planet's chakras.

In his map, the seven great chakras of the Earth are:

Root, Mount Shasta, California (USA): primal vitality, the raw life force of the planet.

Sacral, Lake Titicaca, Peru and Bolivia: the center of the new, from which life is created and renewed.

Solar plexus, Uluru and Kata Tjuta, Australia: the power and vitality that nourish all beings.

Heart, Glastonbury, England: the love and the will of the world.

Throat, the Great Pyramid, Sinai, and the Mount of Olives, the Middle East: the voice of the Earth.

Third eye, a moving center (today, apparently, in England: Shaftesbury): vision, which shifts with the great ages.

Crown, Mount Kailash, Tibet: the sacred summit, point of union with the highest.

And notice the detail that makes my skin prickle: for Coon, Titicaca is the sacral center, the one "where the new is born," the cradle of life that renews itself. It is exactly the same water from which, in Andean memory, Wiracocha emerged to create the sun and the first beings. That same Wiracocha who in this book is one of the names of the center, of the Luminous Self. Lineage and geography coincide once again.

If the Earth is a living body, then we do not live upon it: we live within it, like a cell within an immense being. And no cell thrives by sickening the body that sustains it.

I bring you this not as dogma, but as an invitation to ask yourself better questions, which is, after all, what this book is about. If the Earth truly is a living energetic being, every daily act becomes a conversation with it. And it is worth asking, without guilt but with honesty:

What I consume, what I throw away, what I buy, does it care for the body of which I am a cell, or does it sicken it?

Do I take from the Earth like one who plunders, or like one who receives from a mother and gives back to her?

If I could thank the Pachamama for something today with an act, not with words, what would it be?

The Q'ero elders make despachos, even today: offerings to the earth, a way of giving back and of remembering that one belongs to it, not the other way around. The sacred container

The 7 forces

How to open and close the space

Before any inner work, the sacred space is opened. It is a simple gesture, a ritual like so many we perform throughout the day, but this time with a clear intention: to declare that this will be a space to work with yourself, with respect, commitment and honesty. And within it you place the concrete intention you wish to work on in yourself today. It is not superstition: it is bringing attention and intention, declaring that this moment is different. The seven forces have a structure that is easy to remember: the four power animals (your four levels of perception, the four directions), plus the Munay at the center, plus what is above (the Sun/Sky) and what is below (the Pachamama). Four directions + center + above + below = seven.

Serpent, south. May it help me release what has already fulfilled its time.

Jaguar, west. May it give me courage to move through fear and shadow.

Hummingbird, north. May it guide my journey and nourish my heart.

Eagle-Condor, east. May it lend me its vision and lift me to spirit.

Pachamama, the Earth (below). May it hold me and root me.

Sun / Sky, Inti (above). May it illuminate me and connect me with the heights.

Munay, the heart (center). May love be the center from which everything is done.

You can do it however it arises in you: with a candle, with incense, with flower water, turning toward each direction, or simply in silence, with your hand on your heart. What matters is not the form, but the presence. And when you finish, you close the space: you give thanks to the seven forces and release them. What is opened with respect is closed with gratitude.

The breath and the sound

2 Breaths, 4 Mantras, Bonus

The two breaths embody your two poles. They are not loose techniques: they are the breath of your two groups of archetypes.

Box breathing, magnetic

4-4-4-4: inhale 4, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4. It calms, centers, brings you to coherence and an Alpha state. The breath of the Balancers: to integrate and to still.

Fire breathing, electric

Kapalabhati: short, active exhalations through the nose. It ignites, energizes, awakens the inner fire. The breath of the Radiators: to activate and to project.

The four mantras, one per level

Serpent, body, Om Tare Tuttare Ture Soha. Green Tara, mother of the buddhas: protection, compassion and liberation from suffering.

Jaguar, mind, Ide were were. A chant to Oshun: the sweet waters and the emotions.

Hummingbird, soul, Om Mani Padme Hum. The jewel in the lotus: compassion.

Eagle-Condor, spirit, the Maha-mantra. «Hare Krishna, Hare Krishna, Krishna Krishna, Hare Hare, Hare Rama, Hare Rama, Rama Rama, Hare Hare»: devotion, dissolving into the divine.

Sing aloud or in a whisper, with emotion. The mantra is the Word set to vibrate: sound plus elevated emotion train your state just as heart coherence does. Choose the one for the level you are working on.

Bonus, four gifts from my practice

More than ten years practicing Mahayana Buddhism left me four gifts I want to give you, because they have held me up again and again: three practices and, the fourth, some go-to mantras.

1, 4-7-8 breathing

Inhale through the nose counting 4, hold counting 7 and exhale through the mouth, slow and complete, counting 8. Three or four cycles. The long exhalation is what fires the vagus nerve: it lowers anxiety in seconds. Ideal before sleep or when your body races.

2, The nine rounds, Buddhist purification

Three rounds of three breaths, nine in total, through alternate nostrils. Round 1: inhale through the left, exhale through the right. Round 2: inhale through the right, exhale through the left. Round 3: inhale and exhale through both, rising up the central channel. With each exhalation, release what clouds the mind. It cleanses the channels and leaves it «in neutral», ready to meditate.

And the best of all, the one I use most: the triple filter, before saying or reacting to something, stop and ask yourself three things.

3, The triple filter

1. Is it true?

2. Is it good, does it add, does it bring value?

3. Can I do something about it?

If the balance is yes, act. If not, take it to meditation: there, almost always, it dissolves on its own.

4, My go-to mantras

Beyond the four mantras for each level, I carry with me some chants that accompany me daily. A chant to vitality and longevity: the mantra of White Tara, the one who lengthens and nourishes life (do not confuse her with the Green Tara of the Serpent: they are sisters, with different functions). Om Tare Tu Tare Ture Mama Ah Yuh Pune Jana Putim Kuru Soha.

White Tara, longevity mantra, by Deva Premal and Miten.

And one that honors Mother Earth, the Pachamama that beats throughout this whole book: Gaia Ma, a chant of gratitude to the living planet that holds us.

Gaia Ma, by Deva Premal and Miten.

And the mantra that crowns them all, the Heart Sutra

Above all there is one that, for me, is the most important: the one that closes the Heart Sutra, one of the most recited texts in all of Mahayana Buddhism. It is the perfection of wisdom made sound.

Gate gate paragate parasamgate bodhi svaha

It is usually translated as «gone, gone, gone beyond, gone completely beyond; awakening, svaha» (svaha is the exclamation that closes it, something like «so be it!»). But my mentor, the master Kazuaki Tanahashi, reads it in a way that moves me deeply: where almost everyone says «gone», he says arriving; and where others place a full stop, he places a celebration. In his version it sounds like this: «arriving, arriving, arriving completely, arriving all together: awakening, joy!». It is not a flight from the world, but an arrival; and not in solitude, but together. Tanahashi also reminds us that the mantra is not written in ordinary Sanskrit, but in a form of «incantation», so any translation is barely an approximation: the mantra is not understood, it is vibrated.

What could be more beautiful? «To arrive at awakening all together, with joy» is, said in another language, the dream of this whole book, the 13th Chord sung in Sanskrit.

Practice, the clear Eagle

Questions that open

Your mind answers what you ask it. «Why does this always happen to me?» sets it searching for proof that it always happens to you. «What is this showing me?» sets it searching for the way out. The question does not describe your reality: it directs it. What you focus on expands, and your question is the focus.

The practice

1. Hunt down the hidden question. In discomfort there is always an automatic, dissonant question. Write it down exactly as it sounds.

2. Make the turn. Turn it into one that opens. «What is wrong with me?» → «What part of me is asking for love right now?». «What if I fail?» → «Who do I become if I try?».

3. Run through the four questions of the levels. What do I need to release? (Serpent), What fear is driving this disguised as reason? (Jaguar), What gives me life? (Hummingbird), Who do I want to become? (Eagle-Condor).

4. Close with the two great questions. Who do I want to become? And what path do I feel is right?

5. Ask from coherence, not from noise. Enter Alpha with box breathing, ask the question and do not force the answer: live the question and release it. The answer arrives later, in the shower, not at the desk.

Practice, the way of the Jaguar

Reclaiming your shadow

Every archetype has its light and its shadow. The shadow is not your enemy: it is your denied power. What you do not make conscious, you live out as destiny. It is not about killing the shadow, that is Eagle without Condor: it is about reclaiming it with love, the way of the Jaguar, beyond fear.

The practice

1. Find it by its trail: projection. What irritates you most or what you judge in another person is usually your denied shadow. What quality in others activates me too much? (Or look at the shadow of your archetype in Part III.)

2. Give it a voice. In coherence, ask it: what are you protecting me from? The shadow always protects something, a fear, an old wound. Listen to it; do not fight it.

3. Make the turn. Take the judgment, «she is controlling», and turn it toward yourself: «I control», «I control myself», «I do not control myself». Look for where it is just as true or more so in you, without blame: with loving honesty.

4. Reclaim the hidden energy. Every shadow is a note in dissonance that hides its resonance. Integrated control becomes structure and leadership; over-giving becomes generosity from fullness. Name it and thank it.

5. Integrate it with love. Bring it to the Munay, to the heart. What you love is integrated; what you reject rules you. Close with gratitude to that part of you.

Parallel maps, to go deeper

The four levels of Part II appear, under other names, in traditions that never knew one another. They were not copied: they describe the same ascent. I gather here those parallel maps for anyone who wishes to look at them side by side, without overloading the main path.

Serpent, the body

Inca: Letting go, instinct, survival. Returning to the earth.

Krishnamurti: Discernment: distinguishing the real from the passing.

Perception: The dense plane: perceiving only the material.

Jaguar, mind and emotions

Inca: Moving through fear, reclaiming the shadow.

Krishnamurti: Desirelessness: not being dragged along by wanting.

Perception: The emotional and mental plane: perceiving desire and thought.

Hummingbird, the soul

Inca: Creating, nourishing, following the nectar.

Krishnamurti: Right conduct: living in coherence with what you see.

Perception: The plane of the soul: perceiving meaning and beauty.

Eagle-Condor, the spirit

Inca: Vision, witness, conscious choice.

Krishnamurti: Love: the force that unites everything.

Perception: Unitary consciousness: perceiving the whole as one.

Krishnamurti brought those four qualities together in At the Feet of the Master: discernment, desirelessness, right conduct, and, crowning it all, love. It is, note by note, the same ascent from the Serpent to the Eagle-Condor.

And consciousness, seen with today's eyes, says the same thing. The physicist Thomas Campbell, trained alongside Robert Monroe, proposes that reality has levels we learn to perceive; that research came to be analyzed in a now-declassified CIA report, the Gateway Process (1983). Peter Sage translates it into everyday language: we live on four levels according to where we relate to life from. Life happens to me (to-me): I feel like a victim. Life is made by me (by-me): I take the reins. Life happens through me (through-me): I become a channel. And life is me (as-me): the awareness that all is one. It is the same staircase the brain climbs through its waves, from agitation to stillness: gamma, beta, alpha, and theta. Four animals, four qualities, four levels: a single staircase.
